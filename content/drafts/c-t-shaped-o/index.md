I was never really comfortable with the CTO title.

Not because I couldn't do the job. But because the title means everything and nothing. Grey-haired executives in suits who haven't touched code in decades. Teenagers in Y Combinator batches building their first product. The most senior engineer at a fifty-person company. A head of engineering by another name. A tech lead with a fancier email signature. All CTOs.

CTO in Germany is a particular flavour. The managing directors - Geschäftsführer - tend to come from McKinsey, BCG, or MBA programmes. Engineering backgrounds are rare at the top. Technology gets treated as a cost centre, not a profit centre. I've heard people call them insecure overachievers. Good luck trying to give somebody a pay rise if they've been there more than a year.

The CTO role narrows to match: operational excellence, delivery oversight, risk containment. I can do all of that. But I've always been drawn to product. To design. To branding and growth. To the way marketing and engineering intersect when you're building something people actually want to use.

Depending on the company, I could equally be a CPO. My depth is technical, so I tend to land on the technology side. But product is probably the hardest role of them all when done well, and I have a deep appreciation for great product people who really know their craft.

I've also founded startups, taken risks that most career CTOs wouldn't, and built things from nothing with no safety net. That doesn't fit neatly into any C-suite acronym either. At some point you start rearranging the letters. CTPO. CPTO. C-3PO. None of them fit, which is sort of the point.

## T-Shaped

The idea is simple. You go deep in one discipline - that's the vertical bar. And you develop working knowledge across many others - that's the horizontal bar.

{{< post-image src="t-shaped.png" alt="T-shaped skills: deep in Technology, broad across Product, Design, Brand, and Growth" caption="Deep in one discipline. Broad across many." >}}

Most careers push you toward the vertical. Specialise. Go deeper. Become the expert. That's how you get hired, promoted, recognised. The horizontal bar is treated as a nice-to-have.

But the breakthroughs happen at the intersection. The engineer who understands growth loops builds features that spread. The designer who understands engineering constraints ships faster. The product thinker who understands brand builds things people identify with, not just use.

The horizontal bar is where you see connections that specialists miss.

## Four Senses

These are the four I keep coming back to.

**Product sense.** The ability to identify the right problems to solve, prioritise ruthlessly, and make sound trade-offs. Understanding what's actually valuable.

**Design taste.** Recognising what good looks like - simplicity, clarity, low friction. Starting from the problem, not the interface.

**Marketing sense.** Understanding growth loops: how users discover, share, and spread products. Knowing that viral acquisition without engagement is a leaky bucket.

**Engineering sense.** Technical intuition for what's feasible, what's expensive, and what creates real defensibility. Knowing when AI adds real value versus when traditional software is better.

These aren't four departments. They're four lenses that every product decision should pass through.

## The Honest Part

I've been pushed out a couple of times in my career. Each time, it exposed something structural - about the market, or about myself. After one of those exits, I seriously considered leaving software entirely. Sales. Advisory. Something less tied to platform cycles and investor decisions.

When the role disappears, you have to ask a harder question: if I'm not "CTO", then what am I?

You can't just be broad. Breadth without depth is a bluff - opinions about everything, ability to push nothing forward. That person has perspective but no leverage.

And you can't just be deep. Depth without breadth is a ceiling. The brilliant specialist who can't see how their work connects to the product, the market, the user.

The T-shape is the resolution. Go deep enough in one area to genuinely innovate. Go broad enough everywhere else to know where that innovation connects.

The answer, for me, was that the breadth was the thing. Not the title.

## The Shape

AI changes the equation. It raises the floor of the horizontal bar - giving everyone access to competence across disciplines they're not deep in. Which makes the vertical bar, your real depth, even more valuable as a differentiator.

AI makes breadth easier. Which makes depth more valuable. Which means you need both more than ever.

Job titles are status noise - and not just at the top. Junior, mid-level, senior, staff, principal. The whole ladder is a distraction from the thing that actually matters: a diverse team of people with different strengths who know how to work together. T-shaped people who overlap deliberately rather than defend turf.

The letters in the title don't matter. The shape does.
