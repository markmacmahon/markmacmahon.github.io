Berlin in 2017 felt like the centre of gravity. Venture CTO at BCG Digital Ventures. Momentum everywhere. Talent flowing in. Capital abundant.

The pitch was seductive. Big consultancies had taken the innovator's dilemma and turned it into a service: convince your corporate clients that the only way to innovate is to build a startup outside the mothership. Ring-fence it. Fund it. Move fast. Report back.

BCG Digital Ventures was one of several firms selling this model. And for a while, it worked - or at least it looked like it did.

## Three Weeks

Our client was Volkswagen Financial Services. They wanted a two-sided marketplace for used cars. Before we arrived, they had spent close to a year discussing requirements and trying to figure out how to start.

Our team was three people. Myself as the Venture CTO and lead engineer. A DevOps engineer. A front-end developer. We shipped a working product in three weeks.

We didn't wait for access to Volkswagen's internal systems. We crawled existing used car listings from public websites, ingested the data, and served it to users. We launched under an experimental brand - Upcar.de - and tested marketing to specific parts of Berlin to measure acquisition.

It wasn't polished. It wasn't perfect. But it was real, and it was live, and people were using it. The client was shocked. They had spent a year talking. We had spent three weeks building.

I'd been deep into growth hacking at the time. [Sean Ellis](https://www.linkedin.com/in/seanellis/) had given a talk at BCG Digital Ventures, and his approach to lean experimentation had shaped how I thought about early-stage products. Test before you invest. Measure before you scale. Get something in front of users before you've finished the requirements document.

## The Pressure

Then the pressure came.

The client wanted eighty to ninety engineers. I wanted to go further with less. The lean approach that had delivered results in three weeks didn't fit the corporate expectation of what a serious technology investment looks like. Headcount is how corporates measure commitment. A three-person team that ships fast doesn't look like commitment. It looks like a skunkworks project.

This is the structural tension at the heart of corporate ventures. The startup methodology says: stay small, move fast, validate before scaling. The corporate sponsor says: we're spending millions, show us the machinery.

## Germany

CTO in Germany is a particular flavour.

The managing directors - Geschäftsführer - tend to come from McKinsey, BCG, or MBA programmes. Engineering backgrounds are rare at the top. Technology gets treated as a cost centre, not a profit centre. I've heard people call them insecure overachievers. Good luck trying to give somebody a pay rise if they've been there more than a year.

This shapes everything downstream. When leadership sees technology as a cost to be managed rather than a capability to be invested in, the CTO role narrows. Operational excellence. Delivery oversight. Risk containment. The things that matter most - product intuition, technical strategy, the ability to know when not to scale - get squeezed out.

At Heycar, this tension played out in real time. The lean approach that worked at three people didn't survive contact with the corporate machinery. What started as a fast, focused team became something else.

## The Model

The corporate venture model isn't broken. But it has a failure mode that nobody talks about.

The innovator's dilemma is real. Large companies genuinely struggle to innovate from within. Building outside the mothership can work. But the model fails when the corporate sponsor can't resist applying corporate logic to the startup it created. When headcount becomes the measure of progress. When the process that the startup was built to escape gets reimported through the back door.
